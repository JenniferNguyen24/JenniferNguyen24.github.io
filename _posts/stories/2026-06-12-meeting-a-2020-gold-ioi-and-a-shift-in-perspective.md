# Meeting an IOI Gold Medalist and a Shift in Perspective


This one's going to be pretty different from my usual posts. This is about a memory — one that feels like it just happened, and yet somehow it's already been six years.

Some people know this about me, some don't — I was once part of the HSGS selection training team. Even now, I'm not sure whether to feel happy or sad when I look back on it. Sad, mostly. Because I spent most of that time just trying to survive — dealing with things that had nothing to do with studying, while everyone around me seemed to be doing fine. The boys slamming desks when they couldn't solve a problem, the crude jokes, the comments that made the girls go quiet — all of it is still somewhere in my head, refusing to fully leave. But somewhere inside all of that, there's one memory that's so warm it almost doesn't feel real. The memory of meeting an IOI gold medalist, back in 2020.

---

## *A bias I built before we ever spoke*

Before I joined the team, I'd already heard so much about him. That he didn't care who sat next to him. That he couldn't tell raw meat from cooked. That he never helped anyone who asked, held grudges, kept to himself, thought everyone around him was beneath him. Somewhere along the way, without me even noticing, I'd already decided what kind of person he was — and I'd never even spoken to him once.

It stayed like that until one afternoon, when the team decided to go out together. Someone spotted him walking by and called out, *"Hey X, you coming?"* He didn't even turn his head. It was drizzling lightly, his jacket hood was up, and he walked straight out the gate of building C3 without slowing down for even a second. Something sour rose up in me. The team was already such an unfriendly place — people picking on me over tiny bugs, laughing when I asked for help, copying code while being the first to point out everyone else's mistakes. And what he just did felt like the finishing touch. I was the only girl going out that day, and I already knew I wouldn't be able to talk to anyone. I left that afternoon pretty sure I had him completely figured out.

---

## *Tết School*

Some time passed. I came back to school after recovering from an accident, and the first class I attended was Tết School. He was the one teaching that day — the topic was sqrt decomposition. I'd basically memorized the 2018 competitive programming textbook by then, so the concept itself wasn't new. But my implementation was rough, my thinking was slow, and one by one, everyone around me was ACing problems while I sat there stuck. I didn't know who to ask. I didn't really have anyone to ask. So I took a breath, gathered whatever courage I had left, and called him over.

It was the first time anyone on that team had ever helped me debug.

He leaned over, looked at my code, and instead of sighing or walking away, he just — explained. Quietly. He told me that instead of converting X to (X+1)² by adding 2X+1, he'd subtract X² and add (X+1)² to avoid small errors. It was such a small thing. But I don't know why, something about the way he said it — unhurried, like it was the most normal thing in the world to help someone — made me feel like I could breathe again. He was the gentlest person I'd encountered on that entire team. And he was the most talented. And the first one who actually helped me. More than anything, the way he thought about that problem cracked something open in me — I realized I'd been thinking too rigidly, not letting myself be creative at all. He also called me *em* instead of the flat, distant *mình* that everyone else used. Such a small thing. But I noticed.

*You misjudged him completely, you idiot.*

Later, I made another mistake — I can't remember exactly what it was anymore, six years will do that — but I called him over again, and this time he told me to just replace the variables myself. So I did. By hand. Going through every single line, tracking down every instance of that variable and swapping it out one by one (I'm genuinely laughing as I type this). He stood beside me and watched the whole thing, put his hand over his face, let out a quiet laugh, shook his head — *"You really don't know, do you."* And then he showed me find-and-replace. I felt like I'd just discovered electricity. I could not believe that after an entire year of coding, no one had ever shown me that (still laughing).

The next day, while he was teaching, he drew something on the digital whiteboard, circled it, and erased it. The whole room gasped like they'd witnessed something miraculous — honestly still don't understand why — and he just did this little shrug ¯\_(ツ)_/¯, and everyone burst out laughing. I don't think any of us expected him to be funny. I remember Andy said something that made him crack up too, but I've forgotten what it was. That illness in my second year took a lot of my good memories with it. On the way back I tried to replicate the erasing thing myself, but I couldn't get the circle closed enough. I gave up and walked away. When I came back, he was at the board again, erasing it, doing the same shrug ¯\_(ツ)_/¯. I laughed before I even knew I was going to.

---

## *The last day, and the FFT debug*

His last day teaching was on FFT. I remember finding it so strange — he'd be in the middle of explaining something, and one by one, people would just turn back to their own screens and stop listening. I felt genuinely bad watching that. I barely understood complex numbers at the time, but I sat there and listened to him all the way through. Not everyone gets to be taught by an IOI gold medalist. You don't waste that.

When it came time to code, I thought — *this is probably the last chance I'll have to learn something from him.* And then, for the first time in almost two years, I let myself do something a little reckless. I set my array index to start from -1 and called him over to debug.

He spent nearly fifteen minutes on it. Opened up the terminal, worked through it by hand, the whole thing. Watching him move through a problem was like watching someone think in a language I didn't speak yet but desperately wanted to learn. When he finally found the bug, he smiled — a specific laugh, one I can still hear clearly — and said, *"You already know where it went wrong, don't you?"*

To this day I still wonder if he knew I did it on purpose.

Ah, I remember one tiny moment where our fingers touched when he was checking the code, I felt a flow of electric runs in me that felt so etheral. I thought "So this is the feeling of touching the hands of a genius, wonder how I would feel if one day I get to meet Taylor Swift and touch her hands". 
I'd come into Tết School with almost no passion left for any of this boring and torturing codes, yet I left it wanting to be better. Wanting to keep going. Wanting, eventually, to be as good as him.

---

## *Summer School, and the worst version of me*

Then everything fell apart.

The gap between Tết School and Summer School was Covid-19 — the world had its lowest point, and I had mine too. The illness from before came back. I could barely make it to any sessions, barely get through the day without losing my breath, let alone write any code. By the time Summer School arrived, I had become someone I didn't recognize. I forced myself to change — toxified myself, really — just to be able to exist in that space. I laughed at jokes that weren't funny. I made myself smaller on purpose. I did whatever it took not to be completely alone.

Every time I saw him teaching, I wanted so badly to go back to who I was at Tết School. But I couldn't find her anymore.

And the people who had always put me down — they worshipped him, in their own way. So if they ever noticed me trying to talk to him or get close to him, it would only give them more ammunition. I'd be mocked even more. So one part of me wanted nothing more than to learn from him, and another part kept telling me *don't you dare.* I don't know what image of me he walked away with. But when I look back now, I still can't believe I made it through those three months.

I remember one day I lost my glasses. He sat beside me and read the problems out loud for me. He actually came into the small side room, sat down on the floor with me, and just — read them to me. When Kat brought over her glasses and said to try them, I said I wasn't nearsighted (I was trying to explain that I had astigmatism instead), and he jumped in immediately — *"If you're not nearsighted, why are you wearing glasses?"* — with this expression like he'd been genuinely deceived the entire day. I hadn't been pretending anything (I'm laughing again, writing this). But there was something about that moment — seeing him react like that, so unguarded — that made me laugh too. It was the most expression I'd ever seen from him. And I remember thinking, *I want to be friends with someone like this.* 

But I could already feel that I was running out of time on the team. And so the last time I saw him just — ended. Quietly. Without anything being said. I felt like I had tried everything, and at the same time had done nothing at all. I lost the chance. And somewhere along the way, I lost myself too.

---

## *Looking back*

It's a little strange to be writing all of this.

Probably because I've relapsed again. It's 1:05 in the morning, and all I can do is sit here and smile at these memories. A while back I asked Yến to find out if he even remembered who I was — because I wanted to try, now that I was finally in a place where I could. Independent. No longer being pushed around. Confident enough. But what came back was that he reacted to Yến's message and said he didn't remember me.

Guess that he just happened to help, without any contemplation, maybe I was the only one who was that desperate back then. Who looked at him and found something like a sliver of light in a room that had gone completely dark. And now that things are finally okay, I still carry those moments around like they're something precious.

Please don't read this as something buried or unresolved. I genuinely don't know what I felt back then, and I still don't, not entirely. He existed right on the line between friend and someone who just happened to help — without really being either. Always just a little out of reach.

I've studied enough probability to know the chances of him ever reading this are effectively zero. He doesn't use social media. But I suppose I just wanted to write it down anyway.

I don't really know why.
