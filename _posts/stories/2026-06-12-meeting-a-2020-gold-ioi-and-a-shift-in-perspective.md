# Meeting an IOI Gold Medalist and a Shift in Perspective

This will probably be a very different post from my usual academic ones — this one is about an experience, one that feels like just yesterday, yet was already 6 years ago.

Some people know, some don't — I was once part of the HSGS selection training team. It's an experience I still don't know whether to feel happy or sad about whenever I look back. Sad, mostly, I think, because I always had to deal with so many things happening around me that I could barely focus on studying. The image of people slamming desks and chairs, groaning dramatically because they couldn't solve a problem, making harassing remarks toward the girls, all of it still lingers in my head like it refuses to fully disappear. And yet, buried within all that sadness, there was one memory so warm and beautiful it almost felt unreal — the memory of meeting an IOI gold medalist in 2020.

## *A stereotype about him *

Before I even joined the team, I'd already heard so many stories about him from people at school. That he didn't care who sat next to him, couldn't tell raw meat from cooked, never responded when anyone asked him for anything, held grudges against everyone, isolated himself completely, and looked down on everyone around him. I suppose that's how my bias toward him formed without me even realizing it — funny enough, I hadn't even met or talked to him once at that point.

Things just kept going like that until one day the team decided to go out, and one of the guys, seeing him walk by, asked "X, are you coming?" — he didn't even turn his head. It was drizzling slightly and he had the hood of his jacket up, walking straight out the gate of building C3 without a moment's hesitation. Something uncomfortable rose up in me. The people on the team were already unfriendly enough — always picking on me over some tiny bug, never helping when I asked for it, laughing instead, copying code while having the nerve to call others out for getting problems wrong. And what he just did felt like the peak of it all. I hated that I was the only girl going out that day, knowing I wouldn't be able to talk to anyone. I was pretty sure I'd completely misjudged him.

##* Tet School*

Some time passed, and I came back to school after recovering from an accident. The first class I attended at the training team was Tết School. That day, he was the one teaching — we were learning about sqrt decomposition. I'd practically worn out my copy of the 2018 competitive programming textbook, so the concept itself wasn't new to me, but my implementation was just really rough, and my thinking was slow. While everyone around me was ACing problems one after another, I didn't know who to turn to. With nowhere else to go, I gathered every bit of courage I had and called him over to help debug. It was the first time — genuinely the first time — anyone on that team had ever helped me debug my code. He told me that instead of converting $X$ to $(X+1)^2$, and adding 2X+1, he would subtract $X^2$ and add $(X+1)^2$ to avoid mistakes. I don't even know why, but — he was the gentlest person to me in that entire team. And he was the most talented one. And the first to ever help me. More than anything, his way of thinking cracked something open in me — I realized I'd been thinking too rigidly and never letting myself be creative. He was also the first person there to address me as "anh/em" instead of just "mình" like everyone else did — the others always used "mình," which felt so weirdly cold.

Everything just kept unfolding, and I was standing there like I'd stepped into a world of my own. It really is true — hearing about someone a hundred times is nothing compared to seeing them yourself. You misjudged him completely, you idiot.

Then I made another mistake — I can't quite remember what it was anymore, six years will do that to you — but I remember calling him over again to help fix it, and he told me that for this one, just replace the variables yourself. I genuinely went through my entire code by hand, tracking down every single place I'd used that variable and replacing them one by one (I'm literally laughing as I write this). He stood beside me watching, put his hand over his face, laughed, and shook his head — "You really don't know, do you." And then he showed me the find-and-replace function. It was like a whole new world opened up. I genuinely could not believe that after a full year of coding, that was the first time I'd ever learned about replace (still laughing).

The next day while he was teaching, he drew something on the digital whiteboard, circled it, and erased it — the whole room gasped like they'd just witnessed something extraordinary (still don't understand why), and he did this little shrug ¯\_(ツ)_/¯ and the entire team burst out laughing. I think no one expected him to be funny like that. I remember Andy said something hilarious that made him crack up, but I've completely forgotten what it was — that illness in my second year really did take a lot of my good memories with it. On my way back I tried to practice doing the same erase thing, but I couldn't get it right because my circle wasn't closed enough. I gave up and walked away for a bit, then came back to find him at the board again, erasing it and doing the shrug ¯\_(ツ)_/¯ all over again. I laughed without even meaning to.

## *Last day of Tet School and FFT debug session*

His last day teaching was on FFT. Honestly, I found the other students so strange — he was explaining things so well, yet the moment he started, they'd all turn back to their own problems and stop listening entirely. I actually felt bad for him watching that. I barely understood complex numbers back then, but I still sat there and listened to him all the way through. Not everyone gets the chance to be taught by an IOI gold medalist. When it came time to code, I thought — well, this is probably my last chance to learn from him. Why not stir things up a little? For the first time in two years, I let myself actually try to be friendly with someone on that team. I set my array to start from -1 and called him over to debug. He spent nearly 15 minutes on it — opened up the terminal, debugged by hand, the whole thing. Watching a genius work through a problem in real time, I learned more in those 15 minutes than I could have anywhere else. When he opened the terminal I was completely stunned — what kind of mastery was this? Eventually he found the bug and smiled — a laugh I can still hear clearly even now — and said, "You already know where it went wrong, don't you?" To this day I still wonder whether he knew I did it on purpose, haha. I'm not sure how to put it, but when I came into Tết School I had basically no passion left for coding. After meeting him, I wanted to learn again. I wanted to be as good as him.

## *Summer School and my earthquake *

Then things fell apart. The time between Tết School and Summer School was Covid-19, and the world had its low point, and so did I. The illness from before came back and I could barely make it to any LAH sessions or classes — I literally couldn't breathe, let alone code. By the time Summer School came around, I had turned into a different person. I forced myself to change, toxified myself just to be able to talk to the high-achieving guys on the team, voluntarily putting myself down in order to fit in. Every time I saw him teaching, I genuinely wanted to go back to who I used to be — but I couldn't. I kept warping myself into someone misshapen, going along with tasteless jokes and crude remarks just to not be left out. And the people who used to put me down — they always idolized and admired him, so if they ever saw me trying to get close to him, they'd talk even worse about me. More mockery, more belittling. Part of me desperately wanted to talk to him and learn from him; another part kept telling me not to reach above my station. And so I slowly built up a pretty terrible image of myself in front of him. I have no idea what he thought of me, but looking back now, I still can't believe I survived those three months.

I remember one day during Summer School I lost my glasses. He actually sat beside me and read the problems aloud for me. He even sat in the little room at the training center, on the floor with me. When Kat brought over her glasses and said try these, I said I'm not nearsighted (I was about to say I had astigmatism), and he jumped in immediately — "If you're not nearsighted, why are you wearing glasses?" I mean, it wasn't like I'd been pretending all day (the author is laughing writing this) — he had this look like he'd been deceived, and I found him endearingly funny in that moment. It was the first time I'd seen him show that much expression. I wanted so badly to be friends with people like him — but it felt impossible. I could already see that I wasn't going to be on the team much longer, and yet I still carried this hope of running into him again someday. The last time I saw him ended quietly like that. I felt like I'd tried everything, and at the same time done nothing. I lost my own opportunity, and in the process, lost myself too.

## *My own reflection*

It's strange to be writing all of this out — probably because I've relapsed again. 1:05 in the morning and all I can do is smile to myself remembering those moments. I once asked Yến to check if he even remembered who I was, because I really wanted to try reconnecting — I was independent now, no longer being pushed around or bullied, confident in my own abilities. But the answer I got back was that he reacted to Yến's message and said he didn't remember me at all. I guess I was the only one who, back then, was so desperate — who found him like a sliver of light in a completely dark room — and who, now that things are finally okay, still holds onto it like a beautiful memory. Please don't misread this as some hidden feeling or unresolved attachment. I genuinely don't understand what I felt back then, and I still don't now. He existed perfectly on the boundary between a friend and a benefactor, without really being either — always just out of reach.

Because I've studied probability and read a lot about game theory (mostly because he was the one who taught it and that's why I got into it), I know the probability of him ever reading these words is effectively zero — he doesn't even use social media, haha. But I suppose I just wanted to write it. I don't really know why.
